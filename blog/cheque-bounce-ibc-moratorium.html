Can a Section 138 Case Continue During IBC Moratorium?

By Abhayjeet Singh Rao3 July 20264 min read

A frequently asked question in commercial litigation is whether the moratorium under Section 14 of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC), 2016 protects directors and signatories from cheque bounce proceedings under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act. The short answer is: no — the moratorium protects the corporate debtor, not its directors personally.

The Section 14 Moratorium

When a Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process (CIRP) is admitted under the IBC, Section 14 imposes a moratorium that prohibits:

  • Institution or continuation of suits or proceedings against the corporate debtor
  • Execution of any judgement or order against the corporate debtor
  • Recovery actions against the property of the corporate debtor

The key phrase is "against the corporate debtor." The moratorium is entity-specific — it shields the company, not the individuals behind it.

Directors and Signatories Remain Liable

The Supreme Court in P. Mohanraj v. Shah Brothers Ispat Pvt. Ltd. (2021) held that:

  • Section 138 proceedings against natural persons (directors, partners, signatories) are not stayed by the Section 14 moratorium
  • The moratorium only protects the corporate debtor in its capacity as a juridical entity
  • A director who signed the dishonoured cheque has personal criminal liability that is independent of the company's insolvency

This means that even if the company is undergoing CIRP and the moratorium is in effect, the complainant can continue — or even initiate — Section 138 proceedings against the individual directors.

Practical Implications

For Complainants (Payees)

  • Do not assume that the company's insolvency ends your cheque bounce case. The directors remain liable.
  • File your claim with the Resolution Professional and continue the Section 138 case against the directors — these are parallel remedies.
  • If the company is liquidated and the debt is not fully recovered, the Section 138 conviction can result in compensation from the directors personally.

For Accused Directors

  • The company's insolvency does not automatically protect you from criminal prosecution for cheque dishonour.
  • You cannot argue that the moratorium bars the complaint against you personally.
  • Your best defence lies in the merits of the Section 138 case — was the cheque issued for a legally enforceable debt? Were the procedural requirements (notice, limitation) complied with?
  • Settlement remains an option — negotiating payment of the cheque amount can lead to compounding of the case.

What About Post-Resolution?

If the resolution plan is approved and the company's debts are restructured, the question arises whether the Section 138 case survives. The Supreme Court has indicated that the resolution plan binds all creditors, but the criminal liability of directors — being personal — is not automatically extinguished by the resolution plan unless the plan specifically addresses it and the complainant consents.
