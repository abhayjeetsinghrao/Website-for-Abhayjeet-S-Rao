Section 138 Cheque Bounce: A Complete Legal Guide (2026)

By Abhayjeet Singh Rao29 June 202612 min read

Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881 is one of the most commonly invoked criminal provisions in Indian commercial law. It criminalises the issuance of a cheque that is dishonoured — commonly known as a "bounced cheque" — and provides the payee with a potent remedy that combines criminal prosecution with the practical objective of recovering money.

The Essential Ingredients

For an offence under Section 138 to be established, the following conditions must be met:

  1. The cheque must have been issued for the discharge of a legally enforceable debt or liability
  2. The cheque must have been presented to the bank within the period of its validity (3 months from the date of issue, or the date mentioned on the cheque)
  3. The cheque must have been returned unpaid by the bank due to insufficient funds or because the amount exceeds the arrangement with the bank
  4. The payee must have issued a written demand notice to the drawer within 30 days of receiving the bank's memo of dishonour
  5. The drawer must have failed to pay the cheque amount within 15 days of receiving the demand notice
  6. The complaint must be filed within one month of the expiry of the 15-day period

The Demand Notice: A Mandatory Pre-Condition

The demand notice is mandatory. Without it, the complaint is not maintainable. The notice must:

  • Be in writing
  • Demand payment of the cheque amount
  • Be sent within 30 days of the receipt of the bank's dishonour memo
  • Be sent to the drawer's correct address (as mentioned on the cheque or known to the payee)

The notice is deemed to have been served if it is sent by registered post or speed post to the correct address — even if the drawer refuses to accept it or it is returned with the endorsement "refused" or "addressee not found."

Who Can File the Complaint?

The complaint must be filed by the payee or the holder in due course of the cheque. A complaint by any other person — including a power of attorney holder in their own name — is not maintainable.

Territorial Jurisdiction

Following the Supreme Court's decision in Dashrath Rupsingh Rathod v. State of Maharashtra (2014) as modified by the 2015 amendment, the complaint may be filed at the place where:

  • The cheque is presented for payment — i.e., the branch of the payee's bank
  • The payee's bank returns the cheque unpaid

This gives the complainant the advantage of filing in a court convenient to them, rather than having to travel to the drawer's location.

Trial Procedure

Section 138 cases follow a summary trial procedure for cheques up to a certain value, and a summons trial procedure for larger amounts. The typical stages are:

  1. Filing of complaint with supporting documents (cheque, bank memo, demand notice, postal receipt)
  2. Examination of complainant on oath under Section 200 CrPC
  3. Issuance of process — summons to the accused
  4. Appearance and plea of the accused
  5. Prosecution evidence — examination and cross-examination of prosecution witnesses
  6. Statement of accused under Section 313 CrPC
  7. Defence evidence (if any)
  8. Arguments and judgment

Defences Available to the Accused

Common defences include:

  • No legally enforceable debt: The cheque was not issued for a debt — it was a gift, security, or blank cheque that was misused
  • Notice not received: The demand notice was not served at the correct address (though this is increasingly difficult given the "deemed service" provisions)
  • Complaint beyond limitation: Filed after the one-month window from the expiry of the notice period
  • Payment already made: The amount was paid through other means before or after dishonour
  • Stop payment for valid reasons: The drawer stopped payment because the underlying transaction was vitiated by fraud, misrepresentation, or breach of contract

Interim Compensation Under Section 143A

The 2018 amendment introduced Section 143A, which allows the court to direct the accused to pay interim compensation of up to 20% of the cheque amount to the complainant during the pendency of the case. This is a powerful tool for complainants and a significant pressure point for the accused. The compensation must be paid within 60 days of the order.

Compounding and Settlement

Section 138 cases are compoundable — they can be settled at any stage with the permission of the court. Settlement typically involves:

  • Payment of the cheque amount
  • Interest at an agreed rate
  • Legal costs incurred by the complainant

Courts encourage settlement and may even refer the matter to mediation. From the accused's perspective, settlement avoids the risk of conviction and imprisonment. From the complainant's perspective, it ensures actual recovery rather than a judgment that may be difficult to enforce.

Punishment

On conviction, the court may impose:

  • Imprisonment up to 2 years
  • Fine up to twice the cheque amount
  • Or both

Under Section 357(3) CrPC, the court may also direct payment of compensation to the complainant, which is typically the cheque amount with interest.

Recent Developments

Courts have increasingly used the Section 143A interim compensation mechanism to expedite settlements. The Supreme Court has also clarified that Section 138 proceedings can continue against individual directors even during IBC moratorium against the company, and that dishonour of a cheque issued by a partnership firm can render each partner liable.
