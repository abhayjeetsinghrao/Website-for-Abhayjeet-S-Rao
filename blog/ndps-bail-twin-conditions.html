NDPS Bail: Understanding the Twin Conditions Under Section 37

By Abhayjeet Singh Rao15 June 202610 min read

The Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985 (NDPS Act) imposes one of the most stringent bail regimes in Indian criminal law. For offences involving commercial quantity, Section 37 creates a near-presumption against bail by imposing mandatory "twin conditions" that the accused must satisfy before release can be considered.

The Statutory Framework: Section 37

Section 37(1)(b) of the NDPS Act provides that no person accused of an offence punishable under Sections 19, 24, 27A, or for offences involving commercial quantity under Section 20, 21, 22, 23, or 24 shall be released on bail unless:

(i) The Public Prosecutor has been given an opportunity to oppose the application; and
(ii) Where the Public Prosecutor opposes, the court is satisfied that there are reasonable grounds for believing that the accused is not guilty of such offence and that he is not likely to commit any offence while on bail.

These are cumulative — both must be satisfied. This is a stark departure from the general principle that "bail is the rule, jail is the exception."

What Constitutes Commercial Quantity?

The NDPS Act classifies quantities into three categories, specified in the notification appended to the Act:

  • Small quantity: Below the threshold specified in Column 5 of the notification
  • Intermediate quantity: Between small and commercial quantity — Section 37 does not apply
  • Commercial quantity: Equal to or above the threshold in Column 6 — Section 37 applies in full force

For example, for ganja (marijuana), the small quantity is 1 kg, and the commercial quantity is 20 kg. For heroin, the small quantity is 5 grams and the commercial quantity is 250 grams. For cocaine, small quantity is 2 grams and commercial quantity is 100 grams.

How Courts Apply the Twin Conditions

First Condition: Reasonable Grounds for Believing Not Guilty

This does not require the court to conduct a mini-trial or arrive at a definitive finding of innocence. The Supreme Court in Union of India v. Ram Samujh (1999) held that the court must be satisfied that there are "reasonable grounds" — not certainty — for believing the accused is not guilty. This assessment is based on:

  • Whether the recovery was from the accused's conscious possession
  • Whether the mandatory procedures (search, seizure, sampling under Sections 42, 50, and 52) were followed
  • Whether there are independent witnesses to the recovery
  • Whether the FSL report confirms the substance and quantity
  • Whether there are contradictions in the prosecution case

Second Condition: Not Likely to Commit Any Offence While on Bail

This is a forward-looking assessment. The court considers:

  • The accused's criminal antecedents — are there prior NDPS convictions?
  • The nature of the accusation — was this personal consumption or commercial trafficking?
  • The accused's roots in the community, employment, and family ties
  • Whether there is evidence of a larger network that the accused might rejoin

Key Supreme Court Decisions

  • Union of India v. Ram Samujh (1999): Established that the twin conditions are mandatory and the court cannot bypass them even if the accused has been in custody for an extended period.
  • Tofan Singh v. State of Tamil Nadu (2021): The Constitution Bench held that statements recorded under Section 67 of the NDPS Act are not admissible as confessions. This significantly impacts the first twin condition, as many cases rested heavily on such statements.
  • Mohd. Muslim v. State (NCT of Delhi) (2023): The Supreme Court granted bail in a commercial quantity case after finding serious procedural lapses in compliance with Section 50 (right to be searched before a Magistrate or Gazetted Officer).

Bail for Small and Intermediate Quantities (see also: bail for 25g+ cases)

For small quantity offences (punishable under Section 20(b)(ii)(A) with up to 1 year, or Section 27 for personal consumption with up to 1 year), Section 37 does not apply. Bail is governed by the ordinary provisions of the CrPC/BNSS and is relatively easier to obtain.

For intermediate quantity (between small and commercial), the punishment is up to 10 years, and Section 37 does not apply. Bail is still governed by ordinary provisions, though courts may impose stringent conditions given the nature of the offence.

Challenging the Quantity: A Key Defence Strategy

One of the most effective defence strategies in NDPS cases is to challenge whether the quantity actually qualifies as "commercial." This can be done by:

  • Challenging the sampling procedure — if the sample was not drawn correctly, the FSL report may be unreliable
  • Arguing that the pure content (not the gross weight of the mixture) should determine the quantity — the Supreme Court in Hira Singh v. Union of India (2020) held that for mixtures, the quantity must be determined based on the actual content of the narcotic substance, not the total weight of the mixture
  • Challenging the chain of custody — breaks in the chain from seizure to FSL testing can create reasonable doubt about the quantity

Prolonged Incarceration and Section 37

The Supreme Court has increasingly recognised that even in NDPS cases, prolonged incarceration without trial completion can tilt the balance towards bail. In several recent decisions, the Court has granted bail in commercial quantity cases where the accused has been in custody for 5–7 years and trial is nowhere near completion, holding that the right to a speedy trial under Article 21 must be balanced against the rigour of Section 37.

Practical Considerations in Delhi

In Delhi, NDPS cases are typically investigated by the Narcotics Cell of the Delhi Police or by the NCB (Narcotics Control Bureau). The Special NDPS Courts at Patiala House and Tis Hazari handle these matters. Bail applications are vigorously contested by the prosecution, and the twin conditions are strictly applied. Successful bail applications typically demonstrate clear procedural violations (Section 42/50 non-compliance) or challenge the quantity through the pure-content argument.
