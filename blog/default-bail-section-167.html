Default Bail Under Section 167(2) CrPC: Your Indefeasible Right

By Abhayjeet Singh Rao3 August 202610 min read

Default bail under Section 167(2) CrPC (now Section 187 BNSS) is one of the most significant protections against indefinite incarceration in Indian criminal law. It is an indefeasible right — meaning the court is bound to grant it if the conditions are satisfied, regardless of the seriousness of the offence.

The Statutory Basis

Section 167(2) CrPC provides that if the investigation is not completed and the charge sheet is not filed within the prescribed period, the accused shall be released on bail (the 60/90 day rule) "if he is prepared to and does furnish bail."

The prescribed periods are:

  • 60 days — for offences punishable with imprisonment up to 10 years
  • 90 days — for offences punishable with death, life imprisonment, or imprisonment exceeding 10 years
  • 180 days — for certain offences under UAPA, NIA Act (with court permission to extend)

Why is it "Indefeasible"?

The Supreme Court in Uday Mohanlal Acharya v. State of Maharashtra (2001) held that the right to default bail is indefeasible — it flows from Article 21 of the Constitution (right to life and personal liberty). The State cannot defeat this right by filing an incomplete charge sheet or seeking extensions.

The Critical Timing Question

The interplay between the filing of the charge sheet and the filing of the bail application is the most litigated aspect of default bail:

  • If the bail application is filed before the charge sheet (see detailed timing analysis): the right has been exercised and cannot be defeated by subsequent filing of the charge sheet
  • If the charge sheet is filed before the bail application: the right to default bail is extinguished
  • If both are filed on the same day: the question of timing down to the hour may arise — courts examine which was filed first

The Supreme Court in M. Ravindran v. Intelligence Officer, DRI (2021) reiterated that the right accrues on the expiry of the prescribed period and must be exercised by filing an application. The filing of the charge sheet after the bail application does not extinguish the right.

What Constitutes a Valid Charge Sheet?

An incomplete charge sheet filed merely to defeat the right to default bail may not be treated as compliant. The charge sheet must contain:

  • The names of the accused
  • The nature of the offences
  • The essential facts and evidence
  • The list of witnesses
  • The relevant documents

A bare-bones charge sheet without the FSL report (in NDPS cases, for instance) or without essential witness statements may be challenged as not being a charge sheet in substance.

Default Bail in Special Statutes

  • NDPS Act: The 180-day period applies for commercial quantity offences (Section 36A(4)). The twin conditions of Section 37 do not apply to default bail — the right is unconditional.
  • PMLA: The 60/90-day period applies. The ED cannot circumvent default bail by filing a supplementary complaint.
  • UAPA/NIA: The initial period is 90 days, extendable to 180 days with court permission. The accused must file the bail application before the extension is granted.

Conditions on Default Bail

While the court must grant default bail, it may impose conditions — personal bond, surety, surrender of passport, regular attendance at the police station, and restrictions on travel. These conditions are similar to those imposed on regular bail and are aimed at ensuring the accused's presence during trial.

Practical Strategy

  • Calculate the exact date on which the 60th or 90th day falls from the date of first remand
  • File the bail application on the very day the right accrues — do not wait even a single day
  • File before the charge sheet: If the charge sheet is filed first (even by minutes), the right is lost
  • Be ready with surety: The court will grant bail subject to furnishing bail bonds. Have the surety and bond amounts ready
  • Challenge incomplete charge sheets: If the prosecution files a skeletal charge sheet to defeat default bail, challenge its validity
