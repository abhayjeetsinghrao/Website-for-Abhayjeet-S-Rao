Complete Guide to Anticipatory Bail in India (2026)

By Abhayjeet Singh Rao 7 June 2026 12 min read

Anticipatory bail is one of the most significant safeguards against arbitrary arrest in Indian criminal law. Enshrined originally in Section 438 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 and now in Section 482 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023, it allows a person who apprehends arrest in a non-bailable offence to seek a direction from the court that they shall be released on bail in the event of arrest.

This guide covers the law as it stands in 2026 — the statutory framework, the grounds on which anticipatory bail is granted, the procedure, conditions typically imposed, and the key Supreme Court decisions that shape how courts exercise this discretion.

What is Anticipatory Bail?

Anticipatory bail is a pre-arrest bail. Unlike regular bail — which is sought after a person has been arrested and is in custody — anticipatory bail is sought before arrest, in anticipation of it. The court directs that if the applicant is arrested, they shall be released on bail without being sent to judicial custody.

The rationale is rooted in Article 21 of the Constitution. The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that personal liberty is a fundamental right, and that deprivation of liberty through arrest should be the exception rather than the rule, especially where custodial interrogation is not warranted.

The Statutory Framework

Section 438 CrPC (Pre-2024 Cases)

For cases registered before 1 July 2024, the Code of Criminal Procedure continues to apply. Section 438(1) provides:

"When any person has reason to believe that he may be arrested on an accusation of having committed a non-bailable offence, he may apply to the High Court or the Court of Session for a direction under this section; and that Court may, if it thinks fit, direct that in the event of such arrest, he shall be released on bail."

Section 482 BNSS (Post-1 July 2024 Cases)

The BNSS has replaced the CrPC for all offences registered on or after 1 July 2024. Section 482 substantially re-enacts Section 438, with certain modifications. Notably, it codifies the requirement that the applicant must cooperate with the investigation and must not leave India without the court's permission — conditions that were previously imposed judicially.

Who Can Apply for Anticipatory Bail?

Any person who has reason to believe that they may be arrested for a non-bailable offence may apply. This includes:

  • A person against whom an FIR has been registered but who has not yet been arrested
  • A person who apprehends that an FIR is about to be registered against them
  • A person named in a complaint that is under enquiry

The phrase "reason to believe" does not require the FIR to have been filed. However, in practice, courts are more likely to entertain applications where there is a concrete basis for the apprehension — such as a registered FIR, a notice under Section 41A CrPC / Section 35 BNSS, or credible information of an impending arrest.

Which Court Has Jurisdiction?

Applications for anticipatory bail may be filed before:

  1. The Court of Session having territorial jurisdiction over the place where the offence is alleged to have been committed
  2. The High Court — either as a first application or on appeal against rejection by the Sessions Court

In Delhi, anticipatory bail applications are heard by the Additional Sessions Judges assigned to the relevant district. Appeals lie to the Delhi High Court under Section 439 CrPC / Section 483 BNSS.

Grounds for Anticipatory Bail

The court considers the following factors when deciding whether to grant anticipatory bail:

  1. Nature and gravity of the accusation — Is the offence serious? Does it carry a maximum sentence of death or life imprisonment?
  2. Antecedents of the applicant — Does the applicant have prior criminal history?
  3. Possibility of the applicant fleeing from justice — Is there a flight risk?
  4. Accusation made with the object of injuring or humiliating the applicant — Is the FIR motivated by personal animosity, a property dispute, or a business rivalry?
  5. Whether custodial interrogation is warranted — Has the applicant cooperated with the investigation? Is there recoverable evidence that requires their custody?

The Supreme Court in Sushila Aggarwal v. State (NCT of Delhi) (2020) held that anticipatory bail need not be limited in duration and can continue until the conclusion of the trial, unless the court finds specific reasons to limit it. This was a significant departure from the earlier practice of granting anticipatory bail for limited periods.

Conditions Typically Imposed

Courts routinely impose the following conditions while granting anticipatory bail:

  • The applicant shall cooperate with the investigation and present themselves before the investigating officer as and when required
  • The applicant shall not directly or indirectly make any inducement, threat, or promise to any person acquainted with the facts of the case
  • The applicant shall not leave India without prior permission of the court
  • The applicant shall surrender their passport
  • The applicant shall furnish a personal bond and surety bond in specified amounts
  • The applicant shall mark attendance at the police station at regular intervals

When is Anticipatory Bail Not Available?

Anticipatory bail is not available or is severely restricted in the following categories:

  • NDPS Act (commercial quantity) — The twin conditions under Section 37 of the NDPS Act create a presumption against bail. Courts have generally held that anticipatory bail cannot be granted for commercial quantity offences.
  • SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act — Following the Supreme Court's decision in Prathvi Raj Chauhan v. Union of India (2020), anticipatory bail is generally not available under this Act, though the court left open the possibility in cases of clear abuse.
  • PMLA (Prevention of Money Laundering Act) — The twin conditions under Section 45 PMLA impose a similar restriction to the NDPS Act.
  • NIA Act — For offences under the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act investigated by the NIA, Section 43D(5) imposes restrictive bail conditions comparable to Section 37 NDPS.

Anticipatory Bail vs. Regular Bail vs. Default Bail

It is important to understand how anticipatory bail differs from other forms of bail:

  • Anticipatory bail — sought before arrest; prevents custodial detention
  • Regular bail — sought after arrest; the accused is already in custody and applies for release
  • Default bail (Section 167(2) CrPC / Section 187 BNSS) — an indefeasible right that accrues automatically if the charge sheet is not filed within the statutory period (60 or 90 days)

The Procedure: Step by Step

  1. Drafting the application — The application must set out the facts of the case, the apprehension of arrest, the grounds on which bail is sought, and any conditions the applicant is willing to accept. It should be accompanied by supporting documents, including the FIR (if available), proof of identity, and any material demonstrating cooperation with the investigation.
  2. Filing before the Sessions Court — The application is filed with the court fees and listed for hearing, typically within 1–3 days.
  3. Notice to the prosecution — The court issues notice to the State / IO and may grant interim protection (a "no coercive action" direction) pending hearing.
  4. Arguments — Both sides are heard on the merits. The prosecution may oppose on the grounds that arrest is necessary for investigation.
  5. Order — The court either grants anticipatory bail with conditions, or rejects the application. If rejected, the applicant may approach the High Court.

Key Supreme Court Decisions

  • Gurbaksh Singh Sibbia v. State of Punjab (1980) — The foundational judgment establishing the broad contours of anticipatory bail and holding that it should not be granted in a "blanket" manner but can be granted with conditions.
  • Siddharam Satlingappa Mhetre v. State of Maharashtra (2011) — Laid down detailed parameters for courts to consider and held that anticipatory bail should not be limited in duration as a matter of course.
  • Sushila Aggarwal v. State (NCT of Delhi) (2020) — The Constitution Bench held that anticipatory bail can be granted without any time limit and can continue until the end of trial.
  • Satender Kumar Antil v. CBI (2022) — Directed that courts must follow the "bail is the rule, jail is the exception" principle and that arrest should be a measure of last resort for offences carrying less than seven years imprisonment.

Practical Considerations in Delhi

In the courts of New Delhi, anticipatory bail matters are heard with relative urgency. The typical timeline is:

  • Filing to first hearing: 1–3 working days
  • Interim protection: Often granted on the first date if a prima facie case is made out
  • Final disposal: 2–6 weeks, depending on the complexity of the matter and the prosecution's response

It is essential to engage counsel at the earliest stage — ideally before the FIR is registered or immediately upon learning of it. Early legal intervention can make the difference between seeking anticipatory bail from a position of strength (having cooperated with the investigation) and seeking it under duress (after a non-bailable warrant has been issued).

Frequently Asked Questions

Can anticipatory bail be cancelled?

Yes. The prosecution or complainant may apply for cancellation if the accused misuses the liberty, tampers with evidence, threatens witnesses, or if new facts emerge that were not before the court when bail was granted.

Is anticipatory bail available under the NDPS Act?

Generally not for commercial quantity offences, due to the twin conditions under Section 37. For small quantity offences, courts have entertained applications in limited circumstances, but the threshold remains high.

What is the difference between Section 438 CrPC and Section 482 BNSS?

Section 482 BNSS substantially re-enacts Section 438 CrPC. The key difference is that BNSS codifies conditions that were previously judge-made — such as mandatory cooperation with investigation and a bar on leaving the country without permission.

Can I apply for anticipatory bail before the FIR is filed?

Yes, if you have a reasonable apprehension of arrest. Courts have entertained pre-FIR applications, though a concrete basis for the apprehension (such as a complaint being filed or a police notice) strengthens the application.
