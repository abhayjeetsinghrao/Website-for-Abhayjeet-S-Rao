International Commercial Contracts: Key Clauses Indian Businesses Miss

By Abhayjeet Singh Rao17 August 202610 min read

Indian businesses are increasingly engaging in cross-border transactions — importing goods, exporting services, licensing technology, and entering joint ventures. Yet many enter these transactions with contracts that are either based on domestic templates or accepted from the foreign counterparty without adequate review. The result is often a contract that does not protect the Indian party's interests when things go wrong.

The Clauses That Matter Most

1. Governing Law

The governing law clause determines which country's law applies to interpret the contract. This is not merely academic — it affects:

  • How terms are interpreted (e.g., "best efforts" means different things under English law and Indian law)
  • What remedies are available (specific performance, liquidated damages, penalty clauses)
  • Whether certain clauses are enforceable (non-compete, limitation of liability, indemnity)

Indian businesses often accept English law or New York law governance without understanding the implications. At minimum, ensure your lawyer advises you on how key clauses operate under the chosen law.

2. Dispute Resolution

This is arguably the most important clause. Options include:

  • International arbitration (ICC, SIAC, LCIA) — generally preferred for cross-border contracts because arbitral awards are enforceable in 170+ countries under the New York Convention
  • Litigation in a foreign court — risky for Indian parties due to cost, distance, and unfamiliarity with foreign procedures
  • Litigation in Indian courts — foreign parties may resist, and enforcement of Indian judgments abroad is limited

The strongest position for an Indian business is typically international arbitration seated in Singapore or London, with the substantive law of the contract being the law most favourable to your position.

3. Force Majeure

Post-COVID, force majeure clauses have received unprecedented scrutiny. A well-drafted force majeure clause should:

  • Define force majeure events specifically (not just "acts of God")
  • Include pandemics, government orders, sanctions, and supply chain disruptions
  • Specify the consequences — suspension of obligations, right to terminate, and the notice requirements
  • Include a hardship / renegotiation obligation before termination

4. Limitation of Liability

International contracts frequently contain liability caps that Indian businesses accept without negotiation. Key points:

  • Ensure the cap is proportionate to the contract value
  • Carve out liability for fraud, wilful misconduct, IP infringement, and data breaches from the cap
  • Be aware that under Indian law, a clause that entirely excludes liability may be challenged as unconscionable

5. IP Ownership and Licensing

In technology and services contracts, the question of who owns the intellectual property created during the engagement is critical. Indian IT services companies frequently find that their contracts assign all IP to the client — including background IP and improvements. Negotiate to retain ownership of pre-existing IP and grant a licence only for the specific deliverables.

6. Data Protection

With the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 now in force in India, cross-border data transfers must comply with Indian data protection law. International contracts must address:

  • Data processing terms (as controller or processor)
  • Cross-border transfer mechanisms
  • Breach notification obligations
  • Data localisation requirements (for sensitive categories)

7. Termination

International contracts should clearly specify:

  • Grounds for termination (material breach, insolvency, change of control)
  • Cure periods — how long the defaulting party has to remedy a breach before termination
  • Consequences — return of property, wind-down obligations, survival clauses
  • Termination for convenience — and the compensation payable in such cases

Common Mistakes by Indian Businesses

  1. Accepting the counterparty's template without negotiation — every clause in a foreign template is drafted to protect the drafter
  2. Ignoring the governing law implications — assuming Indian law principles will apply when the contract is governed by English or US law
  3. Agreeing to foreign court jurisdiction — without understanding the cost and practical implications of litigating abroad
  4. Overlooking sanctions compliance — Indian businesses dealing with sanctioned countries or entities may face secondary sanctions risk
  5. Not obtaining local legal advice — an international contract should be reviewed by counsel in both jurisdictions
