Reasonable Reliance in Banking — Sophisticated Parties Cannot Rely on Representations Contradicting Their Signed Contract

Raiffeisen Zentralbank Osterreich AG v. Royal Bank of Scotland plc — [2010] EWHC 1392 (Comm)
Misrepresentation Act 1967, s. 2(1) Misrepresentation Act 1967, s. 2(2) Hedley Byrne & Co v Heller [1964] AC 465

Raiffeisen Zentralbank Osterreich AG (RZB), an Austrian bank, participated as a syndicate lender in a US$150 million facility advanced to a Russian company, Trans-Oil. In the course of negotiations, representatives of Royal Bank of Scotland (who were arranging the facility) made a number of representations to RZB about the identity and nature of the companies that were proposed as co-borrowers and guarantors, in particular about the relationship between Trans-Oil and Novatek, a large Russian gas company. RZB later claimed that these representations were false — that the co-borrowing entities were connected to Trans-Oil rather than Novatek — and that it had been induced to enter the facility in reliance on them. The borrowers defaulted; RZB sued RBS for misrepresentation.

Christopher Clarke J (as he then was) conducted an exhaustive review of the circumstances in which reliance on a pre-contractual representation can be said to be 'reasonable' for the purposes of a misrepresentation claim. He held that reliance will not be reasonable where, by the time the contract was signed, the claimant had available to it from the contractual documentation itself information that was inconsistent with, or that should have put it on inquiry as to the accuracy of, the representation relied upon. RZB was a sophisticated international commercial bank acting through experienced professionals. The credit agreements it signed contained provisions that were inconsistent with the understanding it claimed to have derived from RBS's pre-contractual statements. A competent reader of those agreements, which RZB had ample opportunity and expertise to review, could not have retained a reasonable belief in the representations.

The judgment is now a leading authority on three propositions. First, the standard of reasonableness for assessing reliance adjusts to the sophistication of the claimant — what is reasonable for a retail customer is not necessarily reasonable for an international bank. Second, a representee who fails to read or properly consider the contractual documentation before signing may find that its reliance was unreasonable. Third, representations inconsistent with the terms of the signed agreement may be treated as superseded or qualified by those terms. For practitioners advising on commercial loan disputes, structured finance litigation, and misrepresentation claims arising out of complex financial transactions, Raiffeisen v RBS is an indispensable authority on the pleading and proof of reasonable reliance.
