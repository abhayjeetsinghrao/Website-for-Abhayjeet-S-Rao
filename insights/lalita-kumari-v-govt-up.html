Mandatory FIR Registration — The Police Cannot Refuse to Record a Cognizable Offence

Lalita Kumari v. Government of Uttar Pradesh & Ors. — (2014) 2 SCC 1
S. 154 CrPC S. 156 CrPC S. 482 CrPC Art. 21 Constitution

A Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court of India, answering a reference that had generated conflicting decisions from two-judge benches over many years, held in unequivocal terms that registration of a First Information Report under Section 154 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 is mandatory the moment information received discloses the commission of a cognizable offence. The then-prevalent practice of station house officers conducting informal preliminary inquiries before deciding whether to register an FIR was firmly disapproved. Such an inquiry at the pre-registration stage, the Court held, amounts to an unlawful arrogation of a quasi-judicial function — the determination of whether an offence has been made out is for the courts after trial, not for the police before even recording the complaint.

The Court recognised a narrow and exhaustive exception: where the information received does not on its face disclose a cognizable offence but merely points to the need for an inquiry, a limited preliminary inquiry — ordinarily not exceeding seven days — may be conducted to ascertain whether a cognizable offence is disclosed. The categories where such a preliminary inquiry is permissible are: matrimonial disputes, commercial offences, medical negligence cases, and corruption cases. Outside these categories, and certainly where the information plainly discloses a cognizable offence, no preliminary inquiry is permissible and the FIR must be registered immediately. The Court also held that all information relating to cognizable offences, whether resulting in FIR registration or in a preliminary inquiry, must be mandatorily and meticulously entered in the General Diary/Station Diary.

For practitioners, Lalita Kumari is the indispensable foundation for a writ petition seeking a mandamus directing the police to register an FIR that has been refused. The refusal to register an FIR where the information plainly discloses a cognizable offence also attracts liability under Section 166A of the IPC (failure to record information by a public servant). The decision is consistently followed and has been reiterated by the Court in numerous subsequent matters as representing the settled position on the police’s duty under Section 154 — a duty that is neither discretionary nor dependent on the officer’s subjective assessment of the merits of the complaint.
