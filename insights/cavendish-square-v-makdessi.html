Penalty Clauses Recast — The Legitimate Interest Test Replaces the Genuine Pre-Estimate Rule

Cavendish Square Holding BV v. Makdessi; ParkingEye Ltd v. Beavis — [2015] UKSC 67
Cavendish Square Holding BV v Makdessi [2015] UKSC 67 Dunlop Pneumatic Tyre Co v New Garage [1915] AC 79 UCTA 1977

The UK Supreme Court in Cavendish Square Holding BV v Makdessi, joined with ParkingEye Ltd v Beavis, delivered a landmark recasting of the English law on penalty clauses — a doctrine that had remained substantially unchanged since the House of Lords decision in Dunlop Pneumatic Tyre Co v New Garage & Motor Co Ltd [1915] AC 79. Under Dunlop, a clause was a penalty (and therefore unenforceable) if it stipulated a sum greater than a genuine pre-estimate of the likely loss caused by the breach. That test had been criticised for being ill-adapted to sophisticated commercial contracts where parties negotiate clauses protecting interests beyond mere expectation loss.

Lords Neuberger and Sumption, delivering the majority judgment, held that the Dunlop test was inadequate for modern commercial law. The correct principle is that a clause is penal only if it imposes a detriment on the party in breach that is out of all proportion to any legitimate interest of the innocent party in the enforcement of the primary obligation. The inquiry is twofold: first, does the innocent party have a legitimate interest — financial or otherwise — in the performance of the obligation broken, going beyond mere compensation for breach? Second, is the remedy stipulated for breach extravagant and unconscionable in comparison with that interest? In a negotiated commercial contract between properly advised parties of comparable bargaining power, the court should be very slow to interfere with their assessment of what constitutes adequate protection for the innocent party's legitimate interests.

Applying this test to Makdessi, the Court upheld clauses in a share purchase agreement that denied the seller earn-out payments and permitted the buyer to acquire the seller's remaining shares at a reduced price if the seller competed in breach of restrictive covenants. These provisions protected the buyer's legitimate interest in the goodwill of the acquired business — an interest going well beyond compensation for the loss caused by the seller's breach. In ParkingEye, the Court upheld an £85 parking charge on the basis that the parking company had a legitimate interest in managing its car park efficiently and deterring overstaying. Cavendish Square is now the foundational authority on penalty clauses in English law, and its 'legitimate interest' test applies to all contractual clauses imposing a detriment on a breaching party.
