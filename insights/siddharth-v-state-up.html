Arrest Is Not Automatic on Filing of a Charge Sheet — Section 170 CrPC Does Not Mandate Custody

Siddharth v. State of Uttar Pradesh — (2022) 1 SCC 676
S. 170 CrPC S. 41 CrPC S. 437 CrPC Art. 21 Constitution

The Supreme Court of India addressed a question of considerable practical importance: whether an accused person who has cooperated throughout the investigation and was never arrested must necessarily be produced in custody at the time the investigating officer files the charge sheet under Section 170 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973. The Court held that Section 170 does not impose such a compulsion. The term ‘custody’ in Section 170(1) does not mean physical arrest; it means the presentation of the accused before the court. An accused who is not in physical custody can be presented before the court by the investigating officer at the time of filing the charge sheet without having been arrested.

The Court further held that a criminal court cannot refuse to accept a charge sheet solely on the ground that the accused has not been arrested and produced in physical custody. Such a refusal would be contrary to the scheme of the CrPC and would create a perverse incentive for investigating officers to make unnecessary arrests merely to comply with a procedural formality. The Court also held that the question of whether an accused should be arrested after the charge sheet is filed must be assessed independently, under Section 41 and the bail provisions of the CrPC, on the basis of the actual risk of flight, evidence tampering, or witness intimidation — and not by a mechanical application of the notion that charge sheet = arrest.

Siddharth v. State of Uttar Pradesh is an important safeguard against the punitive use of arrest. It reinforces the principle, consistently articulated from Arnesh Kumar v. State of Bihar (2014) 8 SCC 273 onwards, that arrest must be a necessity and not a routine incident of criminal investigation. For accused persons who have cooperated with investigations and have not been arrested, the judgment provides direct authority to resist an attempt by the prosecution or the court to insist on physical custody as a precondition to the filing of the charge sheet or the commencement of trial. It also supports a bail application in the event that custody is sought post-charge-sheet by reference to the absence of any arrest during investigation as a factor in the bail assessment.
