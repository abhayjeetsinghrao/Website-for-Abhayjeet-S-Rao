Section 67 NDPS Confessions Are Inadmissible — Officers Under the Act Are Police Officers in Law

Toofan Singh v. State of Tamil Nadu — (2021) 4 SCC 1
S. 67 NDPS Act S. 25 Evidence Act S. 27 Evidence Act Art. 20(3) Constitution

A three-judge Bench of the Supreme Court of India resolved a long-standing split of opinion on one of the most practically significant questions in NDPS prosecutions: whether a statement recorded by an officer of the Narcotics Control Bureau, Customs, or a State agency under Section 67 of the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985 is admissible as a confession against the person who made it. The Court held that it is not. Officers of the Central and State agencies vested with powers of investigation and arrest under the NDPS Act are “police officers” within the meaning of Section 25 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872. A confession made to a police officer is inadmissible in evidence under Section 25, and that prohibition applies equally to statements recorded by NDPS officers under Section 67.

The Court reasoned that the test for determining whether an officer is a “police officer” for the purposes of Section 25 is functional rather than formal: if the officer is invested with powers of investigation, search, seizure, and arrest — as NDPS officers plainly are — the officer exercises police functions and the ban on confessions applies. The Court rejected the argument that because NDPS officers operate under a special statute and are not members of the regular police force, the prohibition in Section 25 does not apply. That argument, it held, would defeat the constitutional protection against self-incrimination under Article 20(3) of the Constitution of India, which provides that no person accused of any offence shall be compelled to be a witness against himself.

The practical consequence for NDPS defence work is significant: any conviction resting substantially on a Section 67 statement recorded by an NCB or Customs officer is vulnerable to challenge. The Court in Toofan Singh acknowledged that the decision would affect a large number of pending and concluded prosecutions, but held that the rule of law and the constitutional protection against self-incrimination could not be sacrificed for administrative convenience. The judgment has been consistently applied by the Supreme Court in subsequent cases, including an express direction to the NCB to comply with the ruling. Defence practitioners should examine Section 67 statements in every NDPS brief as a first-order issue going to the root of admissibility.
