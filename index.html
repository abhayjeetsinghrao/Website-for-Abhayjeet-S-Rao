Featured · Bail Law
20 May 2025

Bail is the Rule, Jail is the Exception — Supreme Court Reaffirms the Constitutional Baseline

Satender Kumar Antil v. Central Bureau of Investigation & Anr. — (2022) 10 SCC 51
In one of the most consequential bail pronouncements of the decade, a two-judge Bench comprising Justices S.K. Kaul and M.M. Sundresh issued comprehensive directions to subordinate courts on the proper exercise of jurisdiction under Sections 436, 436A, 437, and 439 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973. The Court's concern arose from the systemic failure of trial courts and High Courts to apply the principle that personal liberty — a right guaranteed under Article 21 of the Constitution — cannot be curtailed as a matter of routine or as a substitute for punishment at the pre-trial stage.

The Court categorised offences into four groups based on the severity of the prescribed punishment, and directed that courts must first determine which category the accused falls in before applying the relevant bail standard. For offences not punishable with death or life imprisonment, the Court held that bail ought ordinarily to be granted unless specific, articulable reasons point to flight risk, tampering of evidence, or danger to public order — and not merely because the prosecution opposes.

Crucially, the Court reiterated that the filing of a charge sheet does not extinguish the bail court's jurisdiction, and that Section 436A — which entitles an undertrial prisoner to bail upon serving one-half of the maximum sentence — must be implemented proactively, including suo motu by prison authorities.
Art. 21 Constitution S. 436 CrPC S. 436A CrPC S. 437 CrPC S. 439 CrPC

Arrest · Procedure
17 May 2025

Arrest Is Not a Foregone Conclusion — Section 41 Imposes a Reasoned Threshold

Arnesh Kumar v. State of Bihar & Anr. — (2014) 8 SCC 273
The Supreme Court, confronted with the widespread misuse of the power of arrest — particularly in matrimonial matters under Section 498-A IPC — laid down binding directions requiring every police officer to apply his mind to the conditions specified in Section 41(1)(b) CrPC before effecting an arrest in cases where the maximum sentence does not exceed seven years.

The Court held that the police officer must record, in writing, his reasons for concluding that arrest is necessary to prevent further offence, to ensure proper investigation, or to prevent the accused from absconding. Magistrates were directed not to authorise detention mechanically and to satisfy themselves, from the reasons recorded, that the conditions for arrest were in fact met. Non-compliance was held to render the arrest illegal and to expose the officer to departmental and legal consequences.
S. 41 CrPC S. 41A CrPC S. 498-A IPC Art. 21 Constitution

NDPS · Bail
13 May 2025

The Twin Conditions Under Section 37 NDPS Are Mandatory — Reasonable Grounds Must Be Recorded

Union of India v. Rattan Mallik @ Habul — (2009) 2 SCC 624
The Supreme Court authoritatively construed Section 37 of the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985, which imposes a non-obstante bar on the grant of bail in commercial quantity cases. The Court held that the two conditions imposed by Section 37(1)(b) are conjunctive and mandatory: the court must first afford the prosecution an opportunity of hearing, and must then form a positive, reasoned satisfaction — not a mere tentative view — that there are reasonable grounds to believe that the accused is not guilty of the alleged offence, and that he is unlikely to commit any offence whilst on bail.

The Court cautioned that these conditions operate as a departure from the general bail jurisprudence and cannot be diluted by recourse to general principles under Sections 437 or 439 CrPC. The word "reasonable grounds" was interpreted to mean something more than a prima facie case — the court must weigh the evidence on record with a degree of seriousness approximating a trial.
S. 37 NDPS Act S. 20 NDPS Act S. 437 CrPC S. 439 CrPC

Criminal Law · Bail Reform
8 June 2026

Bail Is the Rule, Jail Is the Exception — Courts Must Not Routinely Deny Liberty

Satender Kumar Antil v. Central Bureau of Investigation — (2022) 10 SCC 51
The Supreme Court, in a landmark judgment authored by S.K. Kaul, J., sounded a clarion call for bail reform and responded to the persistent over-incarceration of undertrial prisoners across India. The Court observed that the bail system has suffered from a punitive orientation, with pre-trial detention treated as the norm rather than the exception — a practice wholly inconsistent with the constitutional guarantee of personal liberty. Arrest, the Court held, must be founded on clear necessity and must not be mechanically effected upon the registration of an FIR. Where non-custodial measures are adequate, there is no justification for depriving an accused of his liberty before trial.

The Court classified offences into four categories based on the severity of the prescribed punishment and prescribed corresponding approaches to bail applications, emphasising that for offences not governed by special statutes with stringent bail conditions, bail must be considered the rule and refusal the exception. It further directed that bail applications be decided within two weeks and anticipatory bail applications within six weeks. The judgment emphasised that the criminal justice system's purpose is the determination of guilt or innocence after trial — not the punishment of an accused before conviction. Prolonged pre-trial detention violates Article 21 and the presumption of innocence. This decision stands as a foundational charter for bail reform in India.
S. 437 CrPC S. 438 CrPC S. 439 CrPC S. 41 CrPC Art. 21 Constitution

Criminal Law · Bail
5 June 2026

Default Bail: Indefeasible Right on Expiry of Statutory Period

Mohd. Arbaz v. State Of NCT Of Delhi — (2020) 7 SCC 456
In the case of Mohd. Arbaz v. State Of NCT Of Delhi, the Supreme Court addressed the contours of the right to default bail under the proviso to Section 167(2) of the Criminal Procedure Code (CrPC). The factual matrix involved the detainee, Mohd. Arbaz, who had completed the statutory period of 90 days in custody without a chargesheet being filed. The pivotal legal question was whether the expiry of the statutory period under Section 167(2) CrPC automatically entitled the accused to bail, despite an application made thereafter by the prosecution for extension of custody. This legal conundrum brought to the fore the balance between the rights of the accused and the prosecution's duty to conduct a thorough investigation within the stipulated period. The resolution of this issue rests fundamentally on procedural strictness underscored by statutory mandates.

The Supreme Court, in its reasoning, reinforced that the right to default bail is an indefeasible right of the accused under Section 167(2) CrPC, which accrues upon the prosecution's failure to file a chargesheet within 60 or 90 days as applicable. The Court ruled that the filing of an application for extension of custody does not nullify this right unless the extension is granted before the expiry of the statutory period. The Court underscored that mere pendency of an extension application cannot defeat the accrual of this right, thereby emphasizing the principle that procedural compliance is paramount. This judgment reaffirms the procedural protection of personal liberty within the criminal justice framework, highlighting the judiciary's role in safeguarding statutory rights against arbitrary state action.
S. 167(2) CrPC S. 482 CrPC

NDPS · Bail
4 June 2026

Absence of Contraband on the Person Does Not Dispense with the Section 37 NDPS Scrutiny for Bail

Union of India through NCB v. Md. Nawaz Khan — (2021) 10 SCC 100
The accused was charged under Sections 8, 21, 27A, and 29 of the NDPS Act, 1985 in a case involving recovery of a commercial quantity of contraband. The High Court granted him bail principally on the reasoning that no narcotic substance was recovered from his person. The Union, through the Narcotics Control Bureau, challenged the order before the Supreme Court, contending that the High Court had not applied the mandatory twin conditions under Section 37(1)(b)(ii) before releasing the accused.

The Supreme Court, per Dr. D.Y. Chandrachud, J., set aside the bail order. It held that the mere absence of physical possession of the contraband on the person of the accused does not, by itself, satisfy the rigours of Section 37. Where an accused is charged with conspiracy or abetment under Section 29, his role in the wider transaction must be examined. The Court reiterated that bail under the NDPS Act can be granted only where the court records its satisfaction, on reasonable grounds, that the accused is not guilty and is unlikely to commit an offence while on bail — a threshold materially higher than ordinary bail jurisprudence.
S. 37 NDPS Act S. 29 NDPS Act S. 21 NDPS Act S. 439 CrPC

IBC · Resolution Plan
3 June 2026

Once a Resolution Plan Is Approved, All Claims Not Forming Part of It Stand Extinguished

Ghanashyam Mishra & Sons (P) Ltd. v. Edelweiss Asset Reconstruction Co. Ltd. — (2021) 9 SCC 657
Following the corporate insolvency resolution of Orissa Manganese & Minerals Ltd., the Committee of Creditors approved the resolution plan submitted by the appellant. A creditor subsequently sought to enforce a claim that had not been admitted in or provided for under the approved plan. The question before the Supreme Court was whether such residual claims survive the approval of a resolution plan under Section 31 of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016.

The Supreme Court, per B.R. Gavai, J., laid down the "clean slate" principle: once a resolution plan is approved by the Adjudicating Authority under Section 31, it binds the corporate debtor, its creditors, employees, members, guarantors, and all stakeholders — including the Central and State Governments and local authorities. All claims that are not part of the approved plan stand extinguished on the date of approval, and no person may thereafter initiate or continue proceedings in respect of such a claim. The successful resolution applicant takes over the corporate debtor free of undisclosed and unresolved liabilities, ensuring certainty and a fresh start.
S. 31 IBC S. 30 IBC S. 238 IBC

IBC · Operational Debt
2 June 2026

Advance Paid for Goods or Services Is an Operational Debt — A Purchaser Can Be an Operational Creditor

Consolidated Construction Consortium Ltd. v. Hitro Energy Solutions (P) Ltd. — (2022) 7 SCC 164
The appellant had made an advance payment to procure goods and services, which were never supplied, and sought to recover the amount by initiating a corporate insolvency resolution process under Section 9 of the IBC. The NCLAT held that the appellant, being a purchaser rather than a supplier, was not an "operational creditor" and that the advance did not qualify as an "operational debt." The matter reached the Supreme Court.

The Supreme Court, per Dr. D.Y. Chandrachud, J., reversed the NCLAT. It held that the definition of "operational debt" under Section 5(21) is not confined to debts owed to suppliers; a debt arising from an advance payment made for the supply of goods or services falls squarely within it. Consequently, a purchaser who has paid in advance and not received the goods or services is an operational creditor entitled to invoke Section 9. The Court adopted a purposive reading of the Code, holding that the operational character of a debt turns on its nexus with the supply of goods or services, not on the direction of the commercial relationship.
S. 5(21) IBC S. 5(20) IBC S. 9 IBC

Criminal Procedure · Framing of Charges
1 June 2026

The Trial Court Is Not a Mere Post Office — It Must Apply Its Mind While Framing Charges

Ghulam Hassan Beigh v. Mohammad Maqbool Magrey — 2022 SCC OnLine SC 913
In a prosecution for murder, the trial court discharged the accused of the offence under Section 302 IPC and proceeded to frame a charge only for the lesser offence of culpable homicide under Section 304 IPC, relying solely on the post-mortem report. The complainant, the husband of the deceased, challenged this approach. The Supreme Court was called upon to clarify the scope of the trial court's inquiry at the stage of framing charges under Sections 227, 228, and 226 CrPC.

The Supreme Court, in a judgment authored by J.B. Pardiwala, J., held that the trial court is not a "mere post office" and cannot mechanically endorse the police report; it must apply its judicial mind to the material on record and record brief reasons. At the charge-framing stage, the court is to assess whether a prima facie case exists, not to undertake a meticulous appreciation of evidence as at trial. The Court further emphasised that the Public Prosecutor owes a duty under Section 226 to fairly open the prosecution case to the court. Discharging an accused of the graver offence on the strength of medical evidence alone, without permitting the prosecution to lead oral and expert evidence, was held to be impermissible.
S. 227 CrPC S. 228 CrPC S. 226 CrPC S. 302 IPC

RERA · Homebuyer Rights

RERA Operates Retroactively — Homebuyers May Claim Refund with Interest for Delayed Possession

Newtech Promoters & Developers (P) Ltd. v. State of U.P. — 2021 INSC 716
A group of real estate developers challenged the Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, 2016, contending that its application to ongoing projects registered after commencement was impermissibly retrospective, that the Regulatory Authority had no jurisdiction to direct refunds, and that the requirement of pre-deposit for an appeal was unsustainable. The constitutional and jurisdictional validity of these provisions came before the Supreme Court.

The Supreme Court, per Ajay Rastogi, J., upheld the Act. It held that RERA is retroactive — not retrospective — in operation, and validly applies to ongoing projects to protect the legitimate expectations of allottees. The Court affirmed that an allottee has an unqualified right under Section 18 to seek a refund of the amount paid together with interest, or to claim interest for delayed possession, at his option. It clarified the respective jurisdictions of the Authority and the Adjudicating Officer, and upheld the pre-deposit condition for promoter appeals as a reasonable safeguard. The decision firmly tilts the statutory balance in favour of homebuyers.

NI Act · IBC · Director Liability
30 May 2026

Section 14 IBC Moratorium Does Not Bar Section 138 NI Act Proceedings Against a Director

Vishnoo Mittal v. M/s Shakti Trading Company — 2025 INSC 346
The appellant, a director of the corporate debtor, had drawn eleven cheques in favour of the respondent-company pursuant to a super-stockist arrangement. The cheques, totalling approximately Rs. 11,17,326, were dishonoured on 07.07.2018. Shortly thereafter, on 25.07.2018, insolvency proceedings against the corporate debtor commenced and a moratorium under Section 14 of the IBC was imposed. The legal notice under Section 138 NI Act was issued on 06.08.2018 — after the moratorium had come into force — and a complaint was subsequently filed before the Magistrate. The appellant sought quashing of the Section 138 proceedings, contending that the cause of action under the NI Act arose post-moratorium and was therefore barred. The Punjab and Haryana High Court dismissed the petition, prompting the appeal before the Supreme Court.

The Supreme Court, per Justice Sudhanshu Dhulia, dismissed the appeal and reaffirmed the position settled in P. Mohan Raj v. Shah Brothers Ispat Pvt. Ltd. — (2021) 6 SCC 258 — that the moratorium under Section 14 IBC is confined in its operation to the corporate debtor and does not extend to natural persons such as directors. A director who draws a cheque does so in his individual capacity as signatory and remains personally liable under Section 138 NI Act regardless of the insolvency of the company. The Court held that shielding a director from criminal prosecution merely because the corporate debtor is under CIRP would frustrate the purpose of the NI Act and allow individuals to escape liability behind the corporate veil of insolvency proceedings.

NI Act · IBC · Insolvency
29 May 2026

IBC Moratorium Does Not Shield Natural Persons from Section 138 NI Act Prosecution

P. Mohan Raj v. Shah Brothers Ispat Pvt. Ltd. — (2021) 6 SCC 258
A Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court authoritatively resolved the conflict between the moratorium provision under Section 14 of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016 and criminal proceedings under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881. The question was whether the commencement of a Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process (CIRP) against a company bars the continuation or initiation of Section 138 proceedings against the company's directors or other natural persons who drew, accepted, or endorsed the dishonoured cheque. The Court examined the scope of the moratorium — which stays institution of suits, execution of judgments, and enforcement of security interests against the corporate debtor — and whether its protective umbrella extended to co-accused natural persons.

The Court held, unequivocally, that the moratorium under Section 14 IBC is confined to the corporate debtor alone and does not extend to natural persons such as directors, partners, or guarantors. Accordingly, Section 138 NI Act proceedings against such individuals continue unimpeded during the CIRP. The Bench drew a clear distinction between corporate insolvency (Section 14) and personal insolvency (Section 96), noting that only the latter triggers an interim moratorium over debt proceedings against a natural person. The judgment preserves the creditor's right to pursue criminal prosecution against the drawer and endorsers of a dishonoured cheque without being defeated by the corporate insolvency of the drawee company.

Supreme Court Affirms Stringent Norms for NDPS Act Bail

State of Kerala v. Rajesh — (2020) 12 SCC 122
The case of State of Kerala v. Rajesh involved crucial questions regarding the grant of bail under the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985 (NDPS Act). The respondent was accused of trafficking contrary to the stringent provisions of the NDPS Act. The legal question was whether the twin conditions under Section 37 of the NDPS Act—establishment of "reasonable grounds for believing that he is not guilty" and "not likely to commit any offence while on bail"—were fulfilled to allow bail. The High Court had previously granted bail, which was challenged in the Supreme Court for lacking a stringent appraisal of the statutory mandate.

The Supreme Court, reaffirming stringent criteria, reversed the High Court's bail order, emphasizing that the twin conditions under Section 37 NDPS Act are not mere formalities but mandatory thresholds for considering bail. The Court elucidated that a substantive examination of evidence is imperative, thereby setting a significant precedent towards stringent compliance with legislative intent. The decision reinforced the judiciary's pivotal role in balancing individual liberty with societal security, especially concerning serious offences. This landmark judgment signals a reiteration of a stricter approach towards bail applications under the NDPS Act, aligning judicial discretion with legislative objectives.

Supreme Court Refines Capital Sentencing Norms

Manoharan v. State — (2020) 5 SCC 110
In Manoharan v. State, the Supreme Court examined the challenges associated with death penalty sentencing. The case originated when Manoharan, convicted for heinous crimes against minors, was sentenced to death by the trial court, a decision later upheld by the High Court. The core legal question addressed by the Supreme Court was whether the gravity of the crime itself sufficed to impose the death penalty, or whether a detailed consideration of the convict's circumstances was necessary. This presented a critical examination of sentencing jurisprudence, especially in the context of the "rarest of rare" doctrine established in earlier rulings. The Court also assessed whether adequate mitigating factors had been evaluated.

The Supreme Court, in its reasoning, underscored the necessity of considering both aggravating and mitigating factors extensively before passing the capital sentence. It emphasized that a holistic view is essential, reminding the judiciary that the death penalty is irrevocable and must, therefore, adhere strictly to the guidelines of the "rarest of rare" principle. The Court concluded that sentencing must transcend merely the nature of the crime by emphasizing individualized sentence assessments. Such a judicial obligation ensures that justice is not miscarried through undue emphasis on the crime's brutality without considering the convict's broader context. This established a refined standard for adjudicating capital cases.

Circumstantial Evidence
10 May 2025

Five Conditions for Conviction on Circumstantial Evidence — The Panchsheel Still Governs

Sharad Birdhichand Sarda v. State of Maharashtra — (1984) 4 SCC 116
In a prosecution for murder where the case rested entirely on circumstantial evidence, the Supreme Court distilled the governing standard into five conditions — now universally cited as the "Panchsheel of Circumstantial Evidence" — each of which must be individually satisfied before a conviction can be sustained. The circumstances relied upon must be fully established; consistent with the guilt of the accused; conclusive in nature; inconsistent with any other hypothesis of innocence; and collectively of such cogency as to exclude every reasonable doubt.

The Court emphasised that the standard for conviction on circumstantial evidence is not merely that the evidence is consistent with guilt, but that it is inconsistent with any other rational conclusion. A chain of circumstances with a missing link cannot ground a conviction, however suspicious the accused's conduct may appear.

Mitigating Circumstances Cannot Be Brushed Aside — Death Must Remain the Rarest of Rare

Manoj & Ors. v. State of Madhya Pradesh — (2023) 2 SCC 353
Exercising its jurisdiction under Article 136 of the Constitution, the Supreme Court set aside death sentences awarded by the trial court and confirmed by the High Court in a case involving the murder of three members of a family. The Court found that neither the trial court nor the High Court had conducted a proper sentencing hearing in accordance with the guidelines laid down in Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980), and had failed to obtain a report from the prison authorities on the accused's conduct, their socio-economic background, or the possibility of reform.

The Court reaffirmed that sentencing in capital cases is a bifurcated exercise: the court must balance aggravating circumstances against mitigating ones, giving the accused a full and meaningful opportunity to place mitigating material on record. The mere brutality of the offence, without a finding that the accused are beyond reformation, cannot make the case "rarest of rare."

Default Bail · S.167 CrPC
4 May 2025

Default Bail Is an Indefeasible Right — Filing an Incomplete Charge Sheet Does Not Defeat It

M. Ravindran v. Intelligence Officer, Directorate of Revenue Intelligence — (2021) 2 SCC 485
The Supreme Court held that the right to default bail under the proviso to Section 167(2) CrPC is an indefeasible right that accrues the moment the statutory period for filing a charge sheet expires — sixty days for offences punishable with death, life imprisonment or imprisonment for ten years or more; ninety days for all other offences. Once the right accrues and is exercised by way of an application, it cannot be extinguished by the subsequent filing of a charge sheet, even if filed before the court takes up the application.

The Court further held that the prosecution cannot defeat the right by filing a "preliminary" or "incomplete" charge sheet merely to forestall the accrual of the right. A charge sheet filed without the essential documents and materials required by law does not constitute a valid charge sheet for the purpose of Section 167(2), and the right to default bail survives.
